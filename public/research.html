Flow and the Optimal Experience

Have you ever had those moments when you're "in the zone"? You seem to lose track of time and focus on only the task at hand. Athletes experience this often and enter this state when their skills match their opposing player or team, creating a challenge. In 1990, Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, a Hungarian psychologist published the book Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience explaining Flow Psychology. In it, Csikszentmihalyi describes the flow state as so, "the state in which people are so involved in an activity that nothing else seems to matter[...] the experience itself is so enjoyable that people will do it even at great cost".

Csikszentmihalyi uses this image to explain this flow state. In his research, he found that people were considered to be in flow if their perceived skills being used at a given moment were matched by an equally high perception of challenge at that moment. For sake of clarity, say the person being depicted in the graph is a tennis player. When beginning tennis, this person's skills are not very high, nor are the challenges they face- they may play against other novice players. Therefore, they will find themselves in a flow state as depicted by the bottom left grey circle. However if this tennis player's opponent is more skilled than he, his challenges increase while his skills remain the same, thus experiencing anxiety at his lack of skill, moving out of flow (the red circle). However, within the flow state of the first grey circle, if the novice player's skills increase but he continues to play against novice opponents, he will begin to experience boredom as depicted by the purple circle. Regardless of in the red circle (anxiety) or the pruple circle (boredom), the experience is not comfortable, thus forcing the player to make a decision: either increase his challenges, increase his skills, or quit the game all together (in which case he would drop off of the graph completely). If the player choices either of the first two choices, depending on his position at that given time, he will reenter the flow state once again (the upper righthand grey cirle. Despite this, this is also not a sustainable position as one cannot enjoy doing the same thing at the same level for long. We grow either bored or frustrated; and then the desire to enjoy ourselves again pushes us to stretch our skills, or to discover new opportunities for using them.

In his research, Csikszentmihalyi found that the more time a person spent in flow during the week the better the overall quality of their reported experience. These people were especially likely to feel "strong", "active", "creative", "concentrated", "more cheerful", "stronger", "more creative", "satisfied" and "motivated". He explains this state as the "optimal experience"; "In the long run optimal experiences add up to a sense of mastery-- or perhaps better, a sense of participation in determining the content of life."

"Whenever the goal is to improve the quality of life, flow theory can point the way"

Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience

So if flow is so great, why don't people stay in it all the time? The reason is relatively simple: such experiences are not necessarially pleasant at the time they occur. Take the athlete example- Many times when an athlete enters this flow state they are exerting themselves physically to the point of exhaustion. However, the outcome, whether it be beating the opponent or meeting a personal goal is so intrinsicly satisfying and motivating that the experience overall becomes more positive in their mind. So although flow is enjoyable, people cannot stand high levels of challenge at all times and must recover.

Additionally, "when it comes to work, people do not heed their senses. They disregard the quality of their immediate experience and base their motivation instead on the strongly-rooted cultural stereotype of what work is supposed to be like. They think of it as an imposition, a constraint, an infringement of their freedom, and therefore something that should be avoided as much as possible" rather than the means by which to enter a flow state and have the optimal experience
